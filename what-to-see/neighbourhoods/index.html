    Explore the vibes

    Neighbourhoods

    Copenhagen has a remarkable variety and the best way to explore is to stroll around and take in the ambiance.

    Vesterbro

    The conference venue is located in Vesterbro. Once known as the less wealthy part of Copenhagen and home to the red light district in Istedgade, Vesterbro today is one of the hippest areas - equally cool and authentic with a vibrant nightlife. Cafés, bars and restaurants are on every corner interspersed with interesting niche shops. Visit the former meat-packing district which really comes alive at night. On the border to the posh and class district of Frederiksberg, you find Værnedamsgade - a 200m long street that has a distinctly Parisian vibe. Nearby on Tullinsgade, you will find the world’s smallest hotel.

    Nørrebro

    On the opposite side of the lake in front of the hotel, the district of Nørrebro starts. This is a melting pot of cultures from all over the world, and if you are looking for an authentic exotic meal, this is the place to look. Though densely populated, Nørrebro also houses some of the green lungs of the city - for example Assistens Churchyard, which is a favourite spot for locals to hang out in the summer - a bit unusual for a cemetery.

    City Centre

    The historical heart of the city which was founded in 1167 but can be traced back to around 800. Many of the landmark buildings in the city were constructed by King Christian IV in the 1600’s. In his reign, Denmark has a large income from taxing shipping through the sound between Copenhagen and Sweden as well as overseas trading. That wealth was spent on lavish construction works in and around Copenhagen. If you walk from the hotel towards the city centre, you will pass a very colourful building called Palads. It houses a cinema and was decorated by Danish painter and artist Per Arnoldi. A few hundred meters later, you will be near the entrance to the Tivoli Gardens - an amusement park that is one of the top attractions - unfortunately the season ended some time ago, so it is currently closed. Next, you will reach City Hall square. From there you can follow Strøget - the main pedestrian shopping street. This is a great place to go shopping for clothes, souvenirs, Lego for the kids and many other things. To get a real feel for medieval Copenhagen, walk through the narrow cobbled streets Magstræde and Snaregade. Next take the adjacent Kompagnistræde, full of cafés and also home to the Danish Haemophilia Society office. From here, you are close to the Danish Parliament Christiansborg which you can read about under Instagram spots. A bit further you will find the Round Tower - an observatory built in 1642 and used by astronomers such as Ole Rømer. Instead of stairs the tower has a spiral walkway so instruments could be transported by horse and carriage. For a small entrance fee you can walk to the top and enjoy the view over the city’s roofs.
